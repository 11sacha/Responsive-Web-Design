Muhammed Ali

Boxing Heavyweigth World Champion, Muhammad Ali

"Float like a butterfly, sting like a bee.."

Muhammad Ali (1942–2016) was an iconic American boxer, activist, and cultural figure. Born Cassius Clay, he won an Olympic gold medal and became a three-time heavyweight champion. Known for his charisma, he embraced Islam and opposed the Vietnam War, enduring legal battles. His impact extended beyond boxing, influencing civil rights and global perceptions.

  • Early Years (1942–1954): Born Cassius Clay in Louisville, Kentucky.
  • Boxing Beginnings (1954–1960): Discovered boxing at 12, winning numerous titles as an amateur.
  • Olympic Gold (1960): Won the gold medal in the light heavyweight division at the Rome Olympics.
  • Professional Debut (1960): Turned professional and quickly rose through the ranks.
  • Heavyweight Champion (1964): Became the heavyweight champion by defeating Sonny Liston.
  • Conversion to Islam (1964): Embraced Islam and changed his name to Muhammad Ali.
  • Opposition to Vietnam War (1966–1971): Refused military service on religious grounds, facing legal consequences.
  • Return to Boxing (1970): Regained the heavyweight title in a historic fight against Joe Frazier.
  • Later Years (1975–2016): Continued boxing success, retired, battled Parkinson's disease, and became a global peace ambassador.
  • Legacy: Celebrated as one of the greatest boxers and a symbol of resilience, activism, and charisma.
